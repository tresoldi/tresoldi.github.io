I work in **computational historical linguistics**: I build methods to reconstruct the deep
past of languages and the texts that carry them, and I point those methods at families that
most quantitative work has passed over — above all in native South America.

## Phylogenetics

If there is a single thread through my work, it is **phylogenetic inference** — reading
languages, and the manuscripts that transmit texts, as things that evolve, and trying to
recover where they came from out of the traces they leave. It turns up on three fronts.

### Language families

- **Tupí-Guaraní** — Bayesian lexical phylogenetics of one of South America's largest
  families, joining the tree to archaeology and the problem of chronology
  (*PLOS ONE*, 2023).
- **Tupían** — lexical resources and comparison across the wider Tupían stock (TuLeD and
  related datasets).
- **Arawan** — a Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of the Arawan family (in preparation).
- **Uralic** — computational methods for relationships within the Uralic family (ongoing).
- **Macro-Jê** — comparative and phylogenetic work on the Macro-Jê languages.

### Cultural evolution of texts

Under the **[evotext](https://github.com/evotext)** project, the same phylogenetic thinking
is turned on written traditions — **stemmatology** as a phylogenetic problem: recovering how
manuscripts descend from and contaminate one another. Threads include the evolution of
**runestaves**, and stemmatic analyses of literary and patristic corpora (Dante, Chaucer,
and others; ongoing).

### Methods & tools

The inference rests on tools I build and maintain — synthetic data for validation
(`ngesh`), sequence alignment (`malign`), vector representations for trees
(`phylovector`), and data conversion (`panphylo`). See the [software](/software/) page for
the full toolkit.

## Native South American & Indigenous languages

Beyond phylogenetics, I work on the **documentation and computational description** of
Indigenous languages of South America — assembling analysis-ready resources and supporting
community-facing platforms for languages such as Makuráp, Enawenê-Nawê, and Bororo. I would
like the infrastructure to be useful in both directions: for comparison and analysis, and
for the communities whose languages it holds.

## Lexical databases

Much of the above depends on **open, reusable lexical data**, which I both build and
contribute to:

- **[Arca Verborum](https://www.tresoldi.org/arcaverborum)** — analysis-ready datasets from
  Lexibank, denormalised for rapid method development and teaching.
- **GLED** — the Global Lexical Database: basic vocabulary for ~6,500 doculects with
  machine cognates and phonological alignments (*Journal of Open Humanities Data*, 2023).
- Contributions to **Lexibank**, **CLICS**, and the **CLDF** ecosystem for cross-linguistic
  data.

## How I try to work

As much as I can, I work in the open — data, code, and methods where anyone can check them.
A result nobody else can reproduce is really just an assertion with good production values.
And because these are living languages spoken by living people, I try to keep that in view,
rather than treat them as rows in a table.
